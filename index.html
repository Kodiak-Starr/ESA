Methodology Glossary
How the economic figures are calculated

These figures use an IMPLAN Input-Output model — the same standard methodology used by state economic development agencies, the U.S. Bureau of Economic Analysis, and universities. The total impact figure is the sum of three effects:

1
Direct Effects
Jobs, wages, and spending by video game companies themselves — studios, publishers, retailers — operating in the state. These are the industry's own jobs and payroll.
2
Indirect Effects
Jobs at businesses that supply the video game industry — cloud providers, legal firms, staffing agencies, equipment vendors. The supply chain that exists because the industry is here.
3
Induced Effects
Jobs supported when industry workers spend their paychecks locally — on housing, groceries, restaurants, childcare. The everyday economic activity those salaries generate.
Direct + Indirect + Induced = Total Economic Impact
Source: ESA 2026 Economic Impact Report, TEConomy Partners. IMPLAN multi-region input-output model (2025 data). This is the same methodology used by the U.S. Bureau of Economic Analysis and state commerce departments.

Companies & Studios

An industry built on anchor companies and small studios.

The U.S. video game industry runs on a symbiotic relationship between large, globally competitive anchor companies and a vast network of small businesses and independent studios, each essential to the other's success.

Why both matter for policy

Policies that burden the video game industry, through tariffs, access restrictions, or regulatory overreach, don't just affect large corporations. They weaken the platform infrastructure that small studios depend on, reduce the capital large companies invest into independent developers, and raise barriers to entry for the entrepreneurs who are driving the industry's fastest growth. In video games, large and small succeed together.

Remote video game jobs represent a model for distributed economic development. Unlike manufacturing or retail, these are high-wage knowledge jobs that require only broadband access. The Policy Asymmetry

The U.S. video game industry is fundamentally a creative and technology-driven industry. American studios create the video games, the IP, the art, the code, the content. Hardware, largely manufactured in China and Southeast Asia, is the delivery device. Tariffs on hardware don't protect American jobs. They tax American consumers while leaving the studios, developers, and creative workforce untouched.

Who Gets Hurt
· Consumers: face immediate price increases on consoles and peripherals
· Retail workers: 13,099 jobs tied to hardware unit sales volume
· New players: higher entry costs suppress first-time buyers, shrinking the overall gamer base
· Studios & developers: largely insulated, their product is digital

Company Profile
What one tax credit built
Ubisoft Montréal: from 50 employees in a former textile factory to the largest game studio in the world.

Founded in April 1997 as a direct result of Québec's tax credit, Ubisoft Montréal started with 50 people in the Mile End neighborhood's Peck Building. Within a decade it had become the world's largest single game development studio, a title it still holds. The studio is the birthplace of Assassin's Creed, Far Cry, Watch Dogs, Rainbow Six Siege, and Prince of Persia: The Sands of Time.

The studio didn't just create jobs. It seeded an ecosystem. More than 300 other studios followed Ubisoft to Montréal, drawn by the talent pipeline, university programs, and infrastructure that one anchor company helped build. Twenty-eight years later, Ubisoft Montréal is still there, still hiring, still shipping global franchises.

The Efficiency Argument

The video game industry doesn't need to be the largest sector in the economy to matter. With 83,000 direct workers generating $65.5B in GDP, $12.7B in taxes, and $218,000 average wages — while growing at more than 4× the pace of the overall U.S. economy — it is among the highest-return industries the country has. Policies that support its growth, protect its workforce, and preserve its market access compound that return. Policies that restrict it have an outsized fiscal and economic cost relative to the number of workers affected.

Modelling Notes & Known Limitations
Remote workers
14,461 workers (17%) are assigned to states by LinkedIn profile location, not physical office. This distributes induced economic impacts more accurately but may slightly overstate impacts in some states.
Conservative coverage
Not all video game entities are captured. Locally-owned retail, in-game purchase economic activity, and generic "gamification" firms are excluded. Impacts are a conservative lower bound.
Diversified companies
For firms like Microsoft, Sony, and NVIDIA, only the video-game-specific employment portion is counted. TEConomy used LinkedIn Talent Insights keyword queries against named game technologies to isolate game-focused roles.
Esports team estimates
State-level esports team counts (~N teams) are proportional estimates derived from each state's share of NCES game program institutions. Actual counts may differ. These are prefixed "~" throughout the dashboard.
Interstate vs. intrastate impacts
The dashboard shows both intrastate impacts (from in-state companies only) and interstate impacts (state's share of the full U.S. industry's ripple effects). Interstate is always larger. Sum of state intrastate impacts ≠ U.S. total.
Tariff calculator
The tariff impact calculator is an illustrative model applying user-selected tariff % to hardware import value. It does not model price elasticity, substitution effects, or supply chain restructuring. Intended as a policy conversation tool, not a forecast.
